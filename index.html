03 — Water Safety

The Water Isn't Always Safe

Atrazine is the second most-used herbicide in the US — and it runs off farm fields every spring, straight into drinking water. The EPA set a legal limit of 3 ppb. The chart below shows what actually showed up in community water systems across Illinois, Kansas, Ohio, and four other states — measured by the chemical's own manufacturer, Syngenta, under a legal agreement with the EPA.

Source: EPA Atrazine Monitoring Program (AMP), 2010–2019 — epa.gov · Finished drinking water samples only · Red bars = peak reading exceeded EPA's 3 ppb legal limit

Takeaway: Year after year, atrazine spiked above the legal limit in drinking water — documented by Syngenta's own data. The EU banned atrazine in 2003. The US still allows it. When water is chemically unsafe, it's not just an environmental issue — it closes swimming holes, contaminates public pools' source water, and hits low-income communities hardest.
04 — Access & Inequality

Who Gets to Swim?

Swimming is often portrayed as a universal sport — but access to lessons, facilities, and safe water is deeply unequal. The CDC's 2024 Vital Signs report measured exactly this: who knows how to swim, and who has ever had a lesson, broken down by race. The results are stark.

37% of Black adults cannot swim
vs.
15% of all U.S. adults cannot swim

Source: CDC Vital Signs, May 2024 — cdc.gov/vitalsigns/drowning · MMWR Vol. 73, No. 20 · All figures from a nationally representative survey, Oct–Nov 2023

Takeaway: 37% of Black adults cannot swim, compared to 15% of all adults. 72% of Hispanic adults have never taken a swimming lesson. These gaps aren't accidental — they trace back to segregated pools, underfunded community facilities, and the high cost of lessons. Drowning death rates for Black people rose 28% between 2019 and 2021. Climate change, by closing more public pools and making natural water unsafe, threatens to deepen every one of these disparities.

06 — The Future

What Comes Next?

2030s

Pool maintenance costs rise as energy and water become more expensive in drought-prone regions.

2040s

Public pools in underfunded communities close at higher rates. Competitive swimming consolidates around well-resourced institutions.

2050

Swimming becomes a sport defined by access to resources — a luxury rather than a community activity. Who still gets to swim?

Questions to sit with:

  • Will pools become more expensive to maintain and access?
  • Who will still have access to safe swimming environments?
  • How can we design more equitable and sustainable aquatic infrastructure?
07 — Reflection

What should it take
to keep swimming
accessible and sustainable?

Swimming has shaped who I am; I owe it my life and identity. It's given me structure, purpose, and a sense of belonging that I haven't found anywhere else.

Because of that, I think we have a responsibility to protect it, to make it more sustainable, and to ensure others have access to it. If something has given so much to us, we should be thinking about how to give back to it.
